# Policy Memos

This folder contains selected policy memos completed through applied policy research. These memos demonstrate policy analysis, evidence synthesis, problem framing, stakeholder-oriented communication, and actionable recommendations.

## Materials Included

- `Harris Core Project - Make Pickups Inconvenient Again.pdf`: Group policy memo completed for the Harris Core sequence. This project was selected as a finalist. The memo recommends clarifying, publicizing, and increasing enforcement of Chicago’s existing restrictions on large trucks and heavy vehicles on Lake Shore Drive and other boulevards to reduce pedestrian, cyclist, and driver safety risks.
- `Policy memo_Trauma Informed Statewide Report.pdf`: Policy memo recommending an annual statewide report on district-level student trauma and mental health indicators in Illinois.

## Project Summaries

### Harris Core Project: Make Pickups Inconvenient Again

This group memo was selected as a finalist for the Harris Core project at the Harris School of Public Policy. The project recommends increasing enforcement of existing restrictions on heavy vehicles, including pickup trucks, on Chicago boulevards. The memo connects vehicle size and weight to traffic fatality risk, situates the proposal within Vision Zero Chicago, and discusses feasibility, enforcement, public communication, equity implications, and potential unintended effects.

The memo argues that Chicago can reduce traffic fatalities by using existing municipal authority more effectively, rather than creating an entirely new policy infrastructure. It also proposes that publicizing and enforcing the ban could gradually discourage heavy vehicle use in dense urban areas.

### Trauma-Informed Statewide Report

This memo recommends that Illinois publish an annual statewide report synthesizing district-level data on student trauma, adversity, school climate, and mental health. The proposed report would build on existing tools and datasets, including the Adversity Index, 5Essentials Survey, SEL Hubs, REACH initiatives, and public health data.

The memo emphasizes that Illinois already collects relevant data, but lacks a cohesive narrative report that brings these sources together for policymakers, districts, and the public.
